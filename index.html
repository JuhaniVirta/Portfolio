UX PROCESS After acquiring the client, Kitty’s Milkshake Bar, we started off with basic background research: who is our client, what do they do and how do they do it? We did this mainly by online research, finding everything we could about the client.  Since our main task was to study the users (i.e. customers), social media soon became our main source of initial impression of Kitty’s users: Kitty’s had a very positive online presence with a lot of enthusiastic and engaged user base. We decided to take advantage of this and made an online survey to see what made users return to the premises again time after time. In addition, we did the following user research:  1) Brand research on people who had never heard of Kitty’s Milkshake Bar to see how their brand is perceived by non-customers, and  2) On-site interviews with customers, to gain more accurate data of visitors and see if the physically gathered data would give similar results as the online questionnaire data. After analyzing the data, we made customer journey maps, personas and graphs to highlight our most critical findings to the client. The results were consistent across all mediums of data collection and supported the clients understanding of their customers, while providing a few new insights into the composition of their existing user base.

First, we did research. This proved to be a laborious task, since we weren’t able to interview of otherwise contact any of our intended users or have access to the intended use environments. So, we found out everything we could about the topic at hand through all of the channels we could think of and formulated a preliminary idea of what we thought our users might be like and what sort of problems they might want us to address. We then visited Rolls-Royce Marine in Turku (who assigned this project for us) and VTT in Espoo (who have studied ship captains’ work in the past) to fill in the blanks.

Fourth, and final step, was to develop, test and evaluate our prototypes. Since we didn’t have access to the actual intended users, we utilized the expertize of Rolls-Royce Marine’s employees to test our prototypes.  We used an AttrakDiff-questionnaire to measure the usability, appearance and design of the interactive iPad demo; and a modiﬁed Method for Assessment of eXperience (MAX) to evaluate our Radical concept-video.
Radical concept: Rachel (the AI) and  Mikko (the first mate)
Second, based on the data we had, we synthesized the information and  created Personas and Context Scenarios that would help us identify key focus areas that our concepts could address. Finally, we used all of our gathered knowledge about the users and what they do to formulate our Experience Goals. They are answers that we want our concepts to convey to a user that might be worried about their place at the ship, both personally and socially, when automated technologies are becoming more and more prominent.
Mockup of the context scenario for docking
Third, we started to expand on these ideas and formulated our initial concepts.  We used a whirlwind of post-its and other brainstorming activities and came up with two concepts: Applicable: a navigational tool that combines various systems currently used on ship bridges. The aim was to reduce the clutter and amount of separate devices in the work environment of the ofﬁcers. To demonstrate this, we created an interactive iPad demo. Radical: an artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) system that has access to all the sensors and information technologies of the ship, but cannot control any of the operative systems on the ship (such as steering, route planning, cargo loading/unloading etc). This allows the AI to synthesize various sources of information and make plans, schedules and suggestions for the crew that they can utilize in their workﬂow, but not without the crew’s explicit approval.
